🔭 Scientific Practices ▼

Key Concepts

  • Observation: using your 5 senses to collect information. Measured data uses numbers and tools. Observed data describes what you see/hear/smell/touch.
  • Hypothesis: a testable prediction. "If I water the plant more, then it will grow taller." It can be wrong — that's OK! Science learns from wrong answers too.
  • Variable: what you change or measure in an experiment. Change one thing at a time. If you change two things, you can't tell which one caused the result.
  • Repeated trials: running an experiment more than once makes results more reliable. One result could be an accident; three matching results are stronger evidence.
  • Model limitations: a model is a smaller or simpler version of something real. It can't be exactly the same size, made of the same materials, or act exactly like the real thing.
  • Tools: ruler (length) · thermometer (temperature) · balance (mass) · graduated cylinder/beaker (liquid volume) · spring scale (force) · hand lens (magnify) · stopwatch (time).
  • Safety: always wear goggles when using chemicals or heat. Never taste unknown substances. Wash hands after experiments.
  • Plan and conduct investigations; ask questions and make observations
  • Collect, measure, and record data using metric units
  • Organize data into tables, charts, and bar graphs
  • Analyze data and draw reasonable conclusions from evidence
  • Understand that repeated tests increase reliability
  • Know the limitations of models (size, materials, properties)
  • Practice lab safety: goggles, gloves, proper disposal

⚗️ Matter and Energy ▼

Key Concepts

  • Solid: has a definite (fixed) shape and definite volume. Does NOT change shape on its own. Examples: rock, ice, pencil.
  • Liquid: has a definite volume but NO fixed shape — it takes the shape of its container. Examples: water, juice, lava.
  • Gas: has NO fixed shape and NO fixed volume — it expands to fill any container. Examples: air, steam, helium in a balloon.
  • Heating causes: melting (solid → liquid) and evaporation (liquid → gas).
  • Cooling causes: condensation (gas → liquid) and freezing (liquid → solid).
  • Physical properties you can measure: temperature · mass (use a balance) · magnetism (test with a magnet) · ability to sink or float (put in water).
  • Mixture: two or more substances combined. Examples: gravel + sand, trail mix, metal + plastic clips. The substances can still be separated.

⚡ Force, Motion, and Energy ▼

Key Concepts

  • Force: a push or a pull. Forces change the motion of objects — they can make things start moving, stop moving, speed up, slow down, or change direction.
  • Gravity: a force that pulls all objects toward Earth. No matter where you are on Earth, gravity pulls you downward. The bigger the mass, the stronger the gravity.
  • More force = more motion. Throwing a ball harder makes it go farther. Pushing a heavier object requires more force than pushing a lighter one.
  • Magnetism: like poles repel (N↔N, S↔S push apart) · opposite poles attract (N↔S pull together). Magnets attract iron, nickel, cobalt — not plastic or wood.
  • Forms of energy: Mechanical (motion) · Thermal/heat (warmth) · Light (radiant) · Sound (vibrations we hear) · Electrical (flowing through wires).
  • Energy example — guitar: plucking the string creates mechanical energy (movement) → the vibrating string creates sound energy (waves in the air).

🌍 Earth and Space ▼

Key Concepts

  • Planets in order from the Sun (mnemonic): My Very Educated Mother Just Served Us Nachos → Mercury · Venus · Earth · Mars · Jupiter · Saturn · Uranus · Neptune.
  • Earth's motions: Rotation = spinning on its axis → causes day and night (24 hours). Revolution = orbiting the Sun → causes seasons and the year (365 days).
  • Moon: orbits Earth approximately every 28 days. Does NOT produce light — it reflects sunlight.
  • The Sun: a star made of hot gases (hydrogen & helium). Provides light and heat energy that drives the water cycle.
  • Water cycle: Sun heats water → evaporation (liquid → vapor) → vapor cools → condensation (clouds form) → precipitation (rain/snow falls).
  • Soil formation: weathering (wind, water, freeze/thaw breaks rock) + decomposition (dead plants and animals break down) = soil over millions of years.
  • Rapid Earth changes: volcanic eruption · earthquake · landslide · flood · hurricane. These can reshape landscapes quickly.
  • Landforms: mountain (tall, peaked) · hill (shorter, rounded) · valley (low land between hills/mountains) · plain (large flat area) · plateau (high flat land) · delta (fan-shaped deposit at a river's mouth).

🌿 Organisms and Environment ▼

Key Concepts

  • Food chain: shows the flow of energy from one organism to the next. Energy flows in ONE direction: Producer → Primary Consumer → Secondary Consumer → Decomposer.
  • Producers: plants — they make their own food using sunlight (photosynthesis). Consumers eat other organisms. Decomposers (fungi, worms, bacteria) break down dead things.
  • Food web: many overlapping food chains in an ecosystem. Remove one species → the whole web is affected. Example: remove frogs → tadpoles increase, herons decrease.
  • Adaptation: a physical trait or behavior that helps an organism survive in its environment. Examples: polar bear (thick fur for cold) · cactus (stores water for desert) · duck (webbed feet for swimming).
  • Inherited trait: you are BORN with it — it comes from your parents' genes. Examples: eye color, number of legs, flower color.
  • Learned behavior: developed through experience or practice. Examples: dog sitting on command, bird learning a new migration route.
  • Life cycles: Butterfly: egg → caterpillar (larva) → chrysalis (pupa) → butterfly (adult). This is complete metamorphosis. Frog: egg → tadpole → froglet → adult frog. Chicken/sea turtle: egg → hatchling → juvenile → adult.
  • Environmental change: drought → water-storing plants survive, others die. Flood → fire ants form a raft and float to a new location.
